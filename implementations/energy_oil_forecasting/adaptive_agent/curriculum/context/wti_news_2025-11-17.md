In early November 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices remained on a multi-month downward trajectory, heavily pressured by weak global demand and expectations of a looming market surplus. WTI repeatedly tested the critical psychological support level of $60 per barrel. While it briefly dipped below this threshold, prices managed a modest recovery to trade between $59.40 and $60.50 per barrel by mid-month. This downward trend reflects a broader decline in global crude benchmarks, which have fallen to their lowest levels since early 2021.

To combat the bearish market sentiment and prevent a potential global oil glut, the eight key OPEC+ member nations—led by Saudi Arabia and Russia—announced a pivotal policy shift on November 2, 2025. The group agreed to proceed with a highly modest production increase of 137,000 barrels per day (bpd) for December 2025. However, they decided to pause all scheduled production increases for the first quarter of 2026 (January through March). This cautious approach leaves their existing voluntary production cuts of 2.2 million bpd and 1.65 million bpd largely intact, with the group emphasizing full flexibility to pause or reverse these adjustments depending on how market conditions evolve.

Despite the overarching bearish sentiment, geopolitical risks continue to inject sudden volatility into the market. On November 14, 2025, a Ukrainian drone strike targeted the critical Russian export port of Novorossiysk on the Black Sea, damaging a docked vessel and causing fires at the Sheskharis oil terminal. Because this port handles approximately 760,000 bpd of Russian crude and refined products, the attack immediately triggered supply stability concerns and caused WTI prices to spike by up to 2.5% in early trading. This incident highlights the ongoing vulnerability of energy infrastructure amid the Russia-Ukraine conflict, compounded by tightening Western sanctions on major Russian oil entities like Rosneft and Lukoil.

The global demand outlook remains sluggish, keeping a lid on sustained price rallies. In its November 2025 Oil Market Report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) slightly upgraded its 2025 global oil demand growth forecast by 40,000 bpd to 790,000 bpd, driven primarily by the US, China, and Nigeria. However, this growth is still considered modest by historical standards, and the IEA projects a further slowdown to 770,000 bpd in 2026. Meanwhile, the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) released its November Short-Term Energy Outlook (STEO), forecasting that rising global inventories—fueled by expanding non-OPEC+ production from the US, Canada, and Guyana—will push crude prices down further, with Brent projected to average $55 per barrel in 2026. Technical analysts warn that if WTI fails to decisively hold above the $60 mark, it could quickly slide toward the next major support zone near $56 to $58.
