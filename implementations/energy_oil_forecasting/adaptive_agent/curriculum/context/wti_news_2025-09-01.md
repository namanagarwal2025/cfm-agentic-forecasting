### Price Level and Recent Trend
West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices experienced a sharp decline throughout August 2025, ending the month at approximately $64.01 per barrel—representing an 8.5% to 9% drop over the course of the month. This downward trend pushed WTI to its lowest levels since late 2021, marking a roughly 12% year-over-year decline. The bearish price action was primarily driven by a growing global supply glut and a fading geopolitical risk premium, which left the broader energy complex under persistent pressure.

### OPEC+ Production Decisions
Supply dynamics were heavily influenced by OPEC+ decisions to accelerate the unwinding of its voluntary production cuts. Following a 548,000 barrels per day (bpd) increase in August, the eight core OPEC+ nations (including Saudi Arabia and Russia) met virtually on August 3, 2025, and agreed to implement another production hike of 547,000 bpd for September. This rapid rollback of the 2.2 million bpd voluntary cuts, combined with estimated overproduction of about 0.6 million bpd by some member states relative to their targets, significantly boosted global supply expectations and weighed on market sentiment.

### Geopolitical Supply Risks and Non-OPEC+ Supply
While localized geopolitical tensions remained a background consideration, an easing geopolitical backdrop in late August 2025 helped deflate the risk premium previously embedded in crude prices. Meanwhile, non-OPEC+ supply continued to expand robustly, led by the Americas. The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) projected national crude output to reach a record 13.6 million bpd by December 2025, driven by productivity gains in the Permian Basin, further compounding the market's oversupply concerns.

### Demand Outlook
On the demand side, the outlook remained highly pessimistic. The International Energy Agency (IEA) in its August 2025 report projected global oil demand growth of just 680,000 bpd for 2025, citing weaker-than-expected consumption in major economies like China, India, and Brazil. OECD demand was virtually flat, with European and Japanese consumption showing notable weakness. Additionally, market anxieties regarding U.S. trade policies, tariffs, and mixed macroeconomic indicators further dampened global demand expectations.

### Notable Analyst Forecasts
Prominent analyst forecasts reflected this bearish shift. In its August 2025 Short-Term Energy Outlook, the EIA revised its price projections downward, forecasting WTI to average $63.58 per barrel for the full year 2025. Looking ahead to 2026, the EIA warned that massive global inventory builds—expected to exceed 2 million bpd in late 2025 and early 2026—could drag Brent prices down to an average of $51 per barrel, forcing OPEC+ and non-OPEC producers to consider eventual supply cuts to stabilize the market.
