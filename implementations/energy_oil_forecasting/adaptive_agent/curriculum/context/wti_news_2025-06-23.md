### Price Level and Recent Trend
WTI crude oil prices experienced a downward trend through May 2025, averaging $60.94 per barrel (down from $62.96 in April) due to comfortable supply balances and refinery maintenance in Europe. In early June, prices initially hovered in the low $60s. However, a major geopolitical shock on June 13, 2025, reversed this trend. WTI prices spiked sharply, surging from around $67 to over $76 per barrel by mid-June as a risk premium was rapidly priced back into the market.

### OPEC+ Production Decisions
OPEC+ production policy has focused on a gradual unwinding of voluntary supply curbs. Following their virtual meeting on May 31, 2025, the eight core OPEC+ nations (including Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the UAE) agreed to proceed with a scheduled production increase of 411,000 barrels per day (b/d) for July 2025. This followed a similar 411,000 b/d increase implemented for June. The alliance reiterated that these monthly increments remain flexible and can be paused or reversed if market conditions deteriorate.

### Geopolitical Supply Risks
Geopolitical supply risks escalated dramatically on June 13, 2025, when Israel launched airstrikes against targets in Iran, which subsequently retaliated. The strikes targeted Iranian energy infrastructure, including a fire at the South Pars gas field. While physical oil flows from Iran—which exports about 1.7 million b/d of crude—remained largely unaffected immediately after the strikes, the direct conflict raised severe anxieties over potential disruptions to the Strait of Hormuz, a vital chokepoint for 20% of global oil shipments.

### Demand Outlook
The global oil demand outlook for 2025 remains divided but generally cautious due to macroeconomic headwinds and trade tensions. In its June 2025 report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) marginally lowered its 2025 demand growth forecast to 720,000 b/d, pointing to weak second-quarter deliveries in the United States and China. Conversely, OPEC maintained a more bullish stance in its June report, projecting global demand growth of 1.3 million b/d for 2025, heavily supported by transportation and petrochemical demand in non-OECD Asian economies.

### Analyst Forecasts
Prior to the mid-June escalation, prominent financial institutions expected a downward price trajectory for the remainder of the year. J.P. Morgan had forecasted WTI to average $73 per barrel in Q2 2025, declining to $69 in Q3 and $65 in Q4. Similarly, the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) in its June Short-Term Energy Outlook projected a softening price environment. However, following the mid-June military exchanges, market analysts noted that the sudden return of a significant geopolitical risk premium has forced a recalibration of these near-term price models.
