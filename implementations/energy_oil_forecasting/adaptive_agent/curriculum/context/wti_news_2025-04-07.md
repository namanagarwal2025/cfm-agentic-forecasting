WTI crude oil prices experienced a sharp downward trend in late March and early April 2025. After trading in the high $60s for much of March—closing at $68.20 on March 21 and $69.36 on March 28—prices plummeted to $61.99 per barrel for the week ending April 4, 2025. This sudden drop was primarily triggered by escalating trade tensions, notably the U.S. administration's announcement of new global tariffs on April 2, 2025, which stoked fears of a trade war that could severely dampen global economic growth and energy demand.

In terms of supply, OPEC+ shifted its strategy toward gradually returning barrels to the market. On March 3, 2025, eight key members—including Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the UAE—confirmed they would begin unwinding 2.2 million barrels per day (b/d) of voluntary production cuts starting in April. This was followed by a virtual meeting on April 3, 2025, where the group decided to accelerate this unwinding by implementing a production increase of 411,000 b/d in May 2025 (representing three monthly increments combined). OPEC+ maintained that market fundamentals remained healthy, though some delegates and analysts viewed the decision to add supply during a price slide as a gamble to reclaim market share.

Geopolitical risks continued to influence the market, though their upward pressure on prices was increasingly offset by supply growth. The U.S. escalated sanctions on Iran in late March, targeting independent Chinese refiners for the first time to choke off Iranian crude exports. Meanwhile, ongoing tensions in the Middle East involving the Houthis and conflicts in Gaza maintained a baseline risk premium. Conversely, earlier heavy sanctions on Russia had not materially disrupted global oil flows, as Russian crude continued to find steady buyers in China and India at a discount.

The global demand outlook grew increasingly bearish due to macroeconomic headwinds and structural shifts. The International Energy Agency (IEA) in March 2025 projected global oil demand growth of just over 1 million b/d for the year, heavily reliant on China's petrochemical sector. However, sluggish Chinese fuel demand—partially driven by rapid electric vehicle (EV) adoption—and the threat of global tariffs weighed heavily on sentiment. Consequently, the IEA warned of a looming global oil surplus of 600,000 b/d in 2025, which could expand further as OPEC+ unwound its cuts. In line with these fundamentals, prominent analysts like Wood Mackenzie forecast Brent to average $73 per barrel in 2025 (implying WTI in the high $60s), while the World Bank's April 2025 report projected Brent to fall to $64 per barrel in 2025, warning that WTI would hover close to the breakeven threshold required by the average U.S. shale producer to lock in a profit.
