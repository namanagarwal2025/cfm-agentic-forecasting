In early January 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices experienced a notable upward trend, recovering from late-December lows to trade in the mid-$70s range. By January 10, 2025, WTI front-month futures closed at $76.57 per barrel, up from a December average of $69.70 per barrel. This upward momentum was supported by a combination of colder winter weather boosting heating demand and escalating geopolitical supply concerns.

The supply side remains heavily influenced by OPEC+ policy. Following their ministerial meeting on December 5, 2024, the alliance agreed to extend its group-wide 2 million barrels per day (bpd) production cuts through the end of 2026. Additionally, the eight member nations implementing voluntary cuts of 2.2 million bpd agreed to delay the unwinding of these curbs, extending them through March 2025 with a gradual phase-out now scheduled to begin in April 2025. These measures reflect the group's ongoing commitment to defend prices amid expectations of a well-supplied global market.

Geopolitical tensions have intensified, introducing a fresh risk premium to the market. On January 10, 2025, the U.S. government announced expansive new sanctions targeting Russia's oil sector, blacklisting major producers like Gazprom Neft and Surgutneftegaz, alongside over 160 tankers and ship insurers involved in Russian, Iranian, and Venezuelan oil trades. Furthermore, market participants are closely monitoring the potential for the incoming U.S. administration to enforce stricter sanctions on Iranian oil exports. On the domestic front, winter weather risks pose near-term supply threats, particularly with Cushing crude inventories hovering at decade lows.

On the demand side, the International Energy Agency (IEA) recently upgraded its 2025 global oil demand growth forecast to 1.1 million bpd, up from 840,000 bpd in 2024, driven by economic stimulus in China and steady growth in the U.S.. Despite this stronger demand, analysts expect the market to remain comfortably supplied due to robust non-OPEC+ production growth from the U.S., Brazil, Guyana, and Canada. Consequently, prominent institutions like Goldman Sachs forecast Brent crude to average around $76 per barrel in 2025 (trading within a $70–$85 range), while the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) projects Brent to average $74 per barrel, citing high OPEC+ spare capacity as a cap on potential price spikes.
