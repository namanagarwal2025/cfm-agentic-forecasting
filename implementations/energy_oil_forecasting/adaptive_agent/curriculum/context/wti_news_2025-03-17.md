As of mid-March 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices have experienced downward pressure, with spot prices hovering around the $67 per barrel mark (closing at $67.04 on March 7 and $67.18 on March 14). This decline reflects a broader market sell-off that has pushed international benchmarks like Brent crude near three-year lows around $70 per barrel. The downward trend is primarily driven by soured macroeconomic sentiment, escalating global trade tensions, and expectations of a comfortable supply balance in the coming months. However, technical analysts note that WTI recently found strong support near $64.90, prompting a minor rebound toward the $68 resistance level.

In a highly anticipated virtual meeting on March 3, 2025, eight key OPEC+ members—including Saudi Arabia, Russia, the UAE, and Iraq—reaffirmed their plan to begin gradually unwinding 2.2 million barrels per day (b/d) of voluntary production cuts starting April 1, 2025. This phase-out is scheduled to occur over an 18-month period ending in September 2026, alongside a gradual 300,000 b/d quota increase for the UAE. While the group cited "healthy market fundamentals" to justify the decision, they emphasized that the return of these barrels remains "gradual and flexible" and can be paused or reversed if market conditions deteriorate. Additionally, overproducing members have agreed to frontload their compensation cuts, with updated schedules due to the OPEC Secretariat by March 17, 2025.

Geopolitical risks are heavily centered on shifting U.S. trade policies, with the threat of new tariffs and retaliatory measures clouding the global economic outlook. On the supply side, anticipation of tighter U.S. sanctions has prompted Iran and Venezuela to temporarily boost crude flows. Meanwhile, the global demand outlook remains tepid. In its March 13, 2025 report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) slightly downgraded its demand growth estimates for late 2024 and early 2025 to 1.2 million b/d. For the full year 2025, the IEA projects global demand growth to accelerate to just over 1 million b/d, reaching 103.9 million b/d, driven largely by China's petrochemical sector. However, with non-OPEC+ supply (led by the Americas) expected to rise by 1.5 million b/d, the IEA warns of a potential global supply surplus of approximately 600,000 b/d in 2025.

Prominent financial institutions and energy agencies present a cautious outlook for the remainder of the year. The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA), in its March 2025 Short-Term Energy Outlook, expects global inventories to draw down in the second quarter of 2025 due to falling Iranian and Venezuelan output, temporarily lifting Brent prices to $75/b by the third quarter before OPEC+ supply increases apply downward pressure later in the year. Goldman Sachs analysts project Brent to trade within a $70 to $85 per barrel range in 2025, averaging around $76. Analysts widely agree that the massive spare production capacity held by OPEC+ Gulf members—estimated at over 6 million b/d—will act as a firm ceiling on any significant price spikes, even in the event of localized supply disruptions.
