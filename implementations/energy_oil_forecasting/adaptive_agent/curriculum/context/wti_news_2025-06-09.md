In early June 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices continued to experience downward pressure, trading in the low-to-mid $60s per barrel. This followed a bearish trend in May, during which WTI front-month contracts averaged approximately $60.94 to $61.03 per barrel, representing a month-on-month decline of over 3%. The price weakness was primarily driven by a combination of rising supply expectations, trade-related policy uncertainties, and broader macroeconomic concerns.

The downward price momentum was heavily influenced by OPEC+ production policies. On May 31, 2025, eight key OPEC+ members—including Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman—met virtually and agreed to implement a production increase of 411,000 barrels per day (bpd) for July 2025. This decision followed a similar 411,000 bpd increase implemented for June 2025. These adjustments represented a gradual, flexible unwinding of the 2.2 million bpd voluntary cuts initiated in late 2024, though the alliance maintained that these increases could be paused or reversed if market conditions deteriorated.

Despite the prevailing supply glut fears, geopolitical risks continued to provide a modest floor for prices. Market participants remained highly sensitive to tensions in the Middle East and the ongoing Russia-Ukraine conflict. While physical oil flows remained largely uninterrupted in early June, the potential for escalation near critical maritime chokepoints like the Strait of Hormuz kept a risk premium active. Additionally, the threat of more stringent U.S. trade sanctions on major producers like Russia, Iran, and Venezuela remained a key supply-side variable.

On the demand side, the global outlook was characterized by visible signs of deceleration. Data from the first five months of 2025 showed global oil demand rising by just 0.3% year-on-year, a sharp drop from the 1.1% growth recorded during the same period in 2024. This slowdown was largely attributed to weak demand growth in China, structural economic challenges, and the accelerating adoption of electric vehicles (EVs). However, this weakness was partially offset by robust demand growth in India and expectations of a seasonal uptick in gasoline and jet fuel consumption during the summer driving season.

Analyst forecasts in early June 2025 reflected a cautious, range-bound outlook. The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) projected WTI spot prices to average around $63.33 per barrel in the second quarter of 2025, before easing further to $61.68 in the third quarter. Independent research groups, such as Commodia, projected Brent crude to average near $63.99 per barrel for June, closely matching the prevailing futures curve. Meanwhile, commodity strategists at institutions like Saxo Bank noted that while WTI was trading within an established $10 range, upside potential remained strictly capped by the looming supply expansion and softening global demand.
