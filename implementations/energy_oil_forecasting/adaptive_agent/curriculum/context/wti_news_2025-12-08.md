### Price Level and Recent Trend
In late November and early December 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices experienced a steady, controlled downdraft, reflecting a fundamentally heavy market. WTI futures fell from approximately $60.98 per barrel at the end of October to settle around $58.52 by late November, representing a monthly decline of about 4%. Brent crude followed a parallel downward trajectory, trading in the low $62 to $63 range. Technical analysts noted that while WTI faced strong resistance near the $60 to $61 mark, tentative buying interest on dips prevented a disorderly break below key support levels in the $57 to $58 range.

### OPEC+ Production Decisions
To combat seasonal demand weakness and prevent a severe market glut, the eight core OPEC+ nations—Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman—met virtually on November 30, 2025. The group reaffirmed its decision to pause planned production increases for the first quarter of 2026 (January through March). This pause followed a scheduled 137,000 barrels per day (bpd) output increase set for December 2025, which was part of a gradual unwinding of the alliance's voluntary cuts. Additionally, OPEC+ approved a new capacity-assessment mechanism to establish maximum sustainable production baselines starting in 2027.

### Geopolitical Supply Risks
Despite the overarching bearish sentiment, geopolitical tensions continued to inject a risk premium and short-term volatility into the market. A primary focus was the impact of US and UK sanctions targeting major Russian oil producers Rosneft and Lukoil, which threatened to disrupt Russian export flows. Concurrently, market participants closely monitored US diplomatic efforts to broker a peace agreement between Russia and Ukraine, which analysts noted could eventually lead to sanctions relief and introduce more supply to the market. Ongoing frictions in the Middle East and potential shifts in US policy toward Iran also remained key wildcards for global supply security.

### Demand Outlook and Supply Balance
The global demand outlook remained sluggish, failing to keep pace with robust production growth from non-OPEC+ producers, particularly the United States, where domestic output was projected to average a record 13.6 million bpd in 2025. The International Energy Agency (IEA) and the World Bank highlighted that global oil demand growth had slowed to roughly 0.7 to 0.8 million bpd in late 2025, largely due to moderating consumption in China driven by rapid electric vehicle adoption. This weak demand, coupled with rising global inventories, pointed to a widening supply surplus heading into 2026.

### Analyst Forecasts
Prominent financial institutions and energy agencies maintained a bearish outlook for crude prices. In its November Short-Term Energy Outlook (STEO), the US Energy Information Administration (EIA) forecast that rising global inventories would drag crude prices down, expecting Brent to average $69 per barrel in 2025 and fall to $55 per barrel in 2026. Major investment banks aligned with this downward trajectory; Goldman Sachs projected Brent to average $56 per barrel and WTI to average $52 per barrel in 2026. Meanwhile, a Dallas Fed Energy Survey of oil and gas executives revealed an average year-end 2025 WTI price expectation of approximately $63 per barrel, highlighting a cautious consensus among industry players.
