As of late March 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil futures are trading in the high $60s per barrel, with prices hovering around $68.20 on March 21, 2025. This represents a modest recovery and a second consecutive week of gains, rebounding from earlier downward pressure. In February, WTI had averaged $71.21 per barrel, down 5.2% month-on-month, driven by speculative selling as money managers closed bullish positions and increased short positions to their highest levels in over a year. However, recent weeks have seen a price stabilization fueled by tightening supply expectations and geopolitical developments.

On March 3, 2025, eight core OPEC+ member countries (Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman) officially reaffirmed their decision to proceed with a gradual and flexible unwinding of their 2.2 million barrels per day (b/d) voluntary production cuts starting on April 1, 2025. Under this 18-month phase-out plan, which runs through September 2026, the group's production targets will rise by an average of 137,000 b/d each month. This decision also includes a gradual 300,000 b/d quota increase for the UAE. While OPEC+ cited "healthy market fundamentals" for the move, they emphasized that the return of these barrels remains highly flexible and can be paused or reversed depending on evolving market conditions.

Geopolitical tensions continue to provide a floor for oil prices, particularly in the Middle East and through international trade policies. In mid-March 2025, relations between the U.S. and Iran escalated, with U.S. President Donald Trump warning that he would hold Tehran directly accountable for attacks by Houthi rebels on shipping in the Red Sea, prompting ongoing U.S. military strikes in Yemen. Additionally, fresh U.S. sanctions on Iran and potential tariff-related trade policies from the Trump administration have introduced significant supply-side uncertainty. Meanwhile, non-OPEC+ supply continues to expand, with the International Energy Agency (IEA) forecasting that non-OPEC+ production will rise by 1.5 million b/d in 2025, led primarily by the Americas.

The demand outlook remains a point of divergence among major energy agencies. In its March 2025 report, the IEA projected that global oil demand growth will accelerate to just over 1 million b/d in 2025 (reaching 103.9 million b/d), with Asia—particularly China's petrochemical sector—accounting for nearly 60% of these gains. However, the IEA also noted that recent macroeconomic data has come in below expectations, leading to a slight downward revision in recent quarterly growth. In contrast, OPEC remains more bullish, holding its 2025 global oil demand growth forecast steady at 1.4 million b/d in its March Monthly Oil Market Report. Analysts note that the market faces a potential supply surplus of around 600,000 b/d in 2025 due to robust non-OPEC+ output, which continues to weigh on long-term price forecasts.
