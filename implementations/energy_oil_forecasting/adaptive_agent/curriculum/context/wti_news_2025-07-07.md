### Price Level and Recent Trend
As of late June 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices experienced a sharp downward trend, with August futures trading around $64.83 per barrel by June 30. This marked a significant decline from earlier in the month, when prices had surged on geopolitical anxieties and brief optimism surrounding US-China trade talks. Following a sudden de-escalation of Middle East tensions, WTI fell by roughly 17% from its weekly high, testing a critical technical and psychological support zone between $63.60 and $65.00 per barrel.

### OPEC+ Production Decisions
OPEC+ has actively moved forward with plans to gradually restore supply to the market. On May 31, 2025, eight key OPEC+ member nations—including Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the UAE—confirmed they would proceed with a production increase of 411,000 barrels per day (bpd) for July 2025. This adjustment is part of a broader, flexible plan to unwind 2.2 million bpd of voluntary output cuts. The alliance scheduled its next meeting for July 6, 2025, to determine production targets for August, with market participants closely watching for signs of further supply increases.

### Geopolitical Supply Risks
Geopolitical risk premiums heavily influenced the market throughout June 2025. Prices initially spiked mid-month following Israeli airstrikes on Iranian military and nuclear facilities on June 13. However, supply disruption fears quickly dissipated after Israel and Iran agreed to a US-brokered ceasefire around June 24. This sudden diplomatic breakthrough effectively stripped the conflict-driven premium from crude prices, shifting the market's focus back to physical supply and demand fundamentals.

### Demand Outlook and Analyst Forecasts
The global demand outlook remains divided between major energy agencies. In its June 2025 report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) marginally trimmed its 2025 global demand growth forecast to 720,000 bpd, citing weak second-quarter deliveries in the US and China. Conversely, OPEC maintained a more optimistic stance, keeping its 2025 demand growth forecast steady at 1.3 million bpd, driven primarily by non-OECD consumption. Market analysts forecast that with the geopolitical premium gone and OPEC+ unwinding its cuts, WTI prices will face continued downward pressure, with any potential rallies likely capped by bearish supply-demand balances.
