In August 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices have experienced downward pressure, repeatedly gravitating toward a tight band around $63 to $65 per barrel. This level represents a critical threshold, as it aligns closely with the estimated breakeven point for many U.S. shale producers. The downward trajectory follows a softer July, during which the NYMEX WTI front-month contract averaged $67.24 per barrel. Overall, benchmark prices have trended lower throughout the year due to a combination of rising global supply and sluggish demand, bringing crude prices near their lowest levels in over two months.

The primary driver of supply-side pressure is the ongoing unwinding of voluntary production cuts by OPEC+. On August 3, 2025, eight key OPEC+ nations—including Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the UAE—met virtually and reaffirmed their commitment to a gradual rollback of their 2.2 million barrels per day (bpd) voluntary cuts. Following a production increase of 548,000 bpd in August, the group agreed to implement another monthly increment of 547,000 bpd in September 2025. Although OPEC+ maintains other layers of production cuts totaling 3.66 million bpd, the accelerated return of these voluntary barrels has introduced significant bearish sentiment to the market.

While physical oversupply dominates the market narrative, geopolitical risks continue to inject a modest premium and prevent steeper immediate declines. Earlier in the summer, escalating tensions surrounding Iran's nuclear program in mid-June prompted the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) to temporarily raise its near-term price forecasts. Additionally, ongoing conflicts in Eastern Europe and broader Middle Eastern instability keep investors cautious of sudden supply disruptions. However, these geopolitical spikes have been short-lived, as robust non-OPEC+ production—particularly from the U.S., Canada, Brazil, and Guyana—quickly caps any sustained rallies.
