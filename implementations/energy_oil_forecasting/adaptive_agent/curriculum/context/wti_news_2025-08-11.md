As of early August 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices are trading in the mid-to-high $60s range, with the August contract hovering around $66 to $67 per barrel. This represents a slight downward trend from earlier in the summer, as benchmark prices have faced pressure. While strong U.S. summer driving demand initially provided a floor for prices, the broader market sentiment has weakened due to expectations of a comfortably supplied global market heading into the latter half of the year.

A major driver of the shifting supply landscape is the aggressive unwinding of voluntary production cuts by OPEC+. On July 5, 2025, eight key OPEC+ members—including Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, and the UAE—confirmed they would increase collective crude oil production by 548,000 barrels per day (bpd) for August 2025. This decision accelerates the phased rollback of the 2.2 million bpd voluntary cuts initiated in April 2025, leaving only 280,000 bpd of those cuts left to be reinstated. While OPEC+ maintains that these increases are supported by "healthy market fundamentals," the rapid return of barrels has contributed to the recent softening of crude prices.

Geopolitical factors continue to act as a double-edged sword for the oil market. Ongoing security incidents and attacks on shipping in the Red Sea have maintained an active risk premium, keeping traders on alert over potential supply chain disruptions. Conversely, this premium has been partially offset by reports of a potential restart of nuclear talks between the U.S. and Iran, which could eventually ease export restrictions and bring more Iranian crude back to the global market. Additionally, broader macroeconomic uncertainties, such as potential trade tariff policies under the U.S. administration, continue to weigh on market confidence.

The demand outlook remains highly subdued, with the International Energy Agency (IEA) forecasting global oil demand growth of just 700,000 bpd for 2025—the lowest non-pandemic growth rate since 2009. This slowdown is primarily driven by lackluster consumption in emerging markets, particularly China. Consequently, analysts expect a growing market surplus. The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) projects rising global inventories in the fourth quarter of 2025, which could drag Brent prices down toward $62 per barrel. Meanwhile, private forecasters like Commodia present a slightly more optimistic view, forecasting WTI at $70.14 for August 2025, driven by expectations of gradually improving global economic conditions.
