In January and early February 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices experienced a notable recovery, rallying off their December lows and attempting to break out of a multi-month downtrend. By mid-to-late January, WTI front-month futures were trading around the $75 to $76 per barrel range. This upward momentum was supported by declining global inventories, colder winter weather in North America, and a temporary reduction in global supply. However, the market remained volatile, facing some downward pressure later in the month due to escalating trade tensions and macroeconomic uncertainties.

To support market stability, OPEC+ decided in December 2024 to delay the unwinding of its 2.2 million barrels per day (bpd) voluntary production cuts for an additional three months. Under this agreement, these voluntary cuts will remain in place through March 2025, with a gradual monthly phase-out scheduled to begin in April 2025 and run through September 2026. Furthermore, the alliance extended its broader 1.65 million bpd voluntary cuts (originally announced in April 2023) until December 2026. These prolonged supply curbs reflect the group's proactive stance in managing a potential supply overhang driven by rising non-OPEC+ production.

Geopolitical developments continued to inject a risk premium into the market. On January 10, 2025, the United States announced new sanctions targeting Russian oil producers, including Gazprom Neft and Surgutneftegaz, alongside 160 tankers involved in transporting Russian, Iranian, and Venezuelan crude. This was compounded by speculation surrounding a tougher U.S. stance on Iranian oil exports following late-December sanctions on vessels carrying Iranian crude. Additionally, the prospect of new U.S. tariffs on Canadian and Mexican energy imports introduced supply-chain uncertainties, while seasonal winter weather posed potential disruption risks to North American production.

The global demand outlook for 2025 remained a subject of debate among major energy agencies. In its January 2025 Oil Market Report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) projected global oil demand growth to accelerate to 1.05 million bpd in 2025, up from 940,000 bpd in 2024, driven by an improving economic outlook and robust petrochemical demand. Conversely, the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) in its January 2025 Short-Term Energy Outlook (STEO) forecast that WTI crude would average $70.31 per barrel for the full year 2025 (up from its previous estimate of $69.12/b). While the EIA expected prices to remain supported in the first quarter of 2025 due to inventory draws, it projected a downward trend from mid-2025 through 2026 as non-OPEC+ supply growth (led by the U.S., Brazil, Guyana, and Canada) outpaces global demand.
