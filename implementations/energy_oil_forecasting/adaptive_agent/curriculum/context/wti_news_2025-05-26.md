In April and early May 2025, WTI crude oil prices experienced a sharp downward trend, with April marking a nearly 19% decline to close the month around $58.21 per barrel—the steepest monthly loss since late 2021. This plunge was driven by a combination of unexpected supply increases and macroeconomic anxieties, particularly surrounding newly announced U.S. tariffs that stoked fears of a global economic slowdown. However, by mid-May, WTI showed signs of stabilization, rebounding to trade in the $59 to $62 range. This modest recovery was supported by bottom-fishing, a weaker U.S. dollar, and the return of Chinese buyers seeking cheaper crude.

The primary catalyst for the recent price drop was a significant strategic pivot by OPEC+. In April, eight key member nations—including Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the UAE—announced plans to accelerate the unwinding of their 2.2 million barrels per day (bpd) voluntary production cuts. The group decided to implement a production increase of 411,000 bpd starting in May/June 2025, which was nearly triple the previously anticipated monthly increment of 122,000 bpd. This shift from defending price levels to actively returning barrels to the market surprised traders and significantly altered the global supply trajectory.

While the supply outlook has turned increasingly bearish, geopolitical risks continue to inject volatility into the market. Ongoing sanctions against major producers like Russia, Iran, and Venezuela remain a key focal point for potential supply disruptions. Speculation and shifting headlines regarding potential U.S.-Iran diplomatic talks have also caused rapid, short-term price swings. Furthermore, the broader geoeconomic landscape is highly volatile due to the Trump administration's aggressive tariff policies, which have created trade tensions with China and other key partners, leaving market participants highly sensitive to macroeconomic shocks.
