In late November 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices experienced a sharp downward trend, sliding to approximately $57.84 per barrel. This decline was part of a multi-week sell-off that left the U.S. benchmark down more than 13% year-to-date. The downward pressure on prices was amplified by a strengthening U.S. dollar, which made crude more expensive for international buyers, and a broader market sentiment dominated by fears of a growing global supply glut.

To address the oversupplied market, the eight key OPEC+ nations (Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman) met on November 2 and November 30, 2025, deciding to pause their planned production increases for January, February, and March of 2026. This decision halted the gradual unwinding of their voluntary production cuts, which had previously seen output targets rise by 137,000 barrels per day (bpd) in the final months of 2025. The cartel emphasized a cautious approach, retaining the flexibility to adjust or further pause production depending on evolving seasonal demand and market conditions.

Geopolitical risk premiums eased significantly in late November 2025, primarily due to renewed U.S. diplomatic efforts to broker a peace framework between Russia and Ukraine. This potential diplomatic breakthrough alleviated fears of prolonged energy infrastructure disruptions in Eastern Europe, overshadowing a larger-than-expected draw of 3.4 million barrels in U.S. crude inventories. On the supply side, global production had surged by 6.2 million bpd since January 2025, split evenly between OPEC+ and non-OPEC producers like the U.S. and Brazil, with U.S. shale drillers continuing to pump at record levels despite the price squeeze.

Demand projections for 2025 and 2026 remained mixed among major energy agencies. In its November 2025 report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) marginally upgraded its 2025 global oil demand growth forecast to 790,000 bpd, led by consumption in the U.S., China, and Nigeria, while forecasting 770,000 bpd growth for 2026. Conversely, OPEC maintained a more optimistic outlook, projecting demand growth of 1.3 million bpd in 2025 and 1.4 million bpd in 2026. Meanwhile, the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) in its November Short-Term Energy Outlook projected that rising global inventories would continue to outpace demand, forecasting that crude prices would fall through the end of 2025 and average $55 per barrel in 2026.
