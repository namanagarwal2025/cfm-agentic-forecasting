In April 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices experienced a sharp selloff, falling from around $71.48 per barrel at the end of March to approximately $62.05 per barrel by late April, representing a monthly decline of over 13%. WTI futures averaged roughly $61.77 per barrel in April, down from $69.24 in March. This downward trend was heavily pressured by a combination of escalating trade tensions—specifically tariff announcements by the U.S. administration—rising non-OPEC+ supply, and the unwinding of OPEC+ production cuts, which triggered bearish sentiment and fears of a global economic slowdown.

OPEC+ has embarked on a gradual unwinding of its 2.2 million barrels per day (b/d) of voluntary production cuts, which began in April 2025. Following an April 3, 2025 meeting, the eight core OPEC+ members (Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, and Oman) implemented a production increase of 411,000 b/d for May 2025. On May 3, 2025, the group met virtually again and agreed to implement another production adjustment of 411,000 b/d for June 2025, citing "healthy market fundamentals" and low oil inventories, while maintaining the flexibility to pause or reverse these increases if market conditions shift.

While geopolitical risks in regions like Yemen and Ukraine continue to introduce volatility, their immediate price premium has been tempered by expectations of robust supply growth outside of OPEC+. On the demand side, the outlook has weakened. The International Energy Agency (IEA) in its April 2025 report revised its 2025 global oil demand growth forecast down by 300,000 b/d to 730,000 b/d, citing the negative economic impact of escalating trade tensions. Similarly, OPEC revised its demand growth forecast downward by 100,000 b/d for both 2025 and 2026, bringing its growth estimate to 1.3 million b/d, as trade wars and sluggish demand in Asia (partially due to electric vehicle penetration) weigh on consumption.

Analysts and international institutions project a comfortably supplied market for the remainder of 2025. The IMF's April 2025 World Economic Outlook indicated that futures markets expect oil prices to average around $66.90 per barrel in 2025 (a 15.5% decline). Goldman Sachs analysts forecast Brent to trade in a $70–$85 range for 2025, averaging $76, noting that high spare capacity among OPEC+ producers (estimated at over 6 million b/d in the Gulf) will likely cap any significant price spikes. Meanwhile, robust non-OPEC+ supply growth continues to offset OPEC+ cuts, keeping the overall market balance tilted toward a surplus.
