### Price Level and Recent Trend
WTI crude oil prices have experienced a sustained downward trend heading into mid-October 2025. The benchmark is averaging around $60.89 per barrel, representing a 5% decline month-on-month and a 15% drop year-on-year from October 2024 levels. WTI has plunged more than 25% from its June highs and over 13% from its September peak, testing key technical support levels in the high-$50s to low-$60s range. This downward momentum reflects a broader bearish sentiment across global energy benchmarks, with Brent crude similarly declining to the mid-$60s.

### OPEC+ Production Decisions
In a strategic pivot toward reclaiming market share, eight key OPEC+ members—including Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, and the UAE—agreed during virtual meetings in September and October 2025 to gradually boost production. The alliance decided to implement a modest output increase of 137,000 barrels per day (bpd) starting in October, with another 137,000 bpd scheduled for November. This decision marks the beginning of unwinding the 1.65 million bpd voluntary cuts originally announced in April 2023. Analysts note that by choosing to raise output rather than pause, OPEC+ is signaling a willingness to prioritize volume and market share over price defense, even in a softer price environment.

### Geopolitical Supply Risks
Geopolitical factors continue to inject volatility into the market. Fresh trade tensions between the U.S. and China—sparked by threats of 100% tariffs on Chinese goods following Beijing's rare-earth export restrictions—have stoked global economic slowdown fears and weighed heavily on crude futures. On the supply side, the market is closely monitoring the potential for tighter U.S. sanctions on Russian oil flows (targeting entities like Rosneft and Lukoil) and EU restrictions on Russian energy imports. Additionally, while Iran's export capacity has remained robust at over 2 million bpd, the threat of renewed sanctions or regional escalation keeps a baseline risk premium active.

### Demand Outlook and Analyst Forecasts
The demand outlook remains highly subdued, reinforcing expectations of a looming global supply glut. In its October 2025 Oil Market Report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) revised its 2025 global oil demand growth forecast downward to 710,000 bpd, citing a harsh macroeconomic climate and rapid transport electrification. With global supply rising to 108 million bpd in September—driven by both OPEC+ increases and expanding non-OPEC+ production from the U.S., Brazil, and Guyana—the IEA projects a significant supply overhang. Market analysts warn that while technical support may temporarily slow WTI's decline, the combination of rising supply and sluggish demand will likely keep downward pressure on prices in the near term.
