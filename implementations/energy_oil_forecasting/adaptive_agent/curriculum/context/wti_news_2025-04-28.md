In April 2025, the global crude oil market experienced a sharp selloff, with benchmark prices plunging to their lowest levels in four years. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil futures exhibited significant downward pressure, trading around $61.33 per barrel in mid-April before recovering slightly to close at $63.08 per barrel by April 25. This bearish trend was triggered in early April when a sudden escalation in trade tensions and the prospect of higher supplies from OPEC+ sent shockwaves through the energy complex, causing benchmark crude prices to drop 14% in the first week of the month alone.

OPEC+ decided to proceed with its plan to gradually unwind its 2.2 million barrels per day (b/d) of voluntary production cuts starting April 1, 2025. Reaffirming this decision in their April 3, 2025 meeting, the eight participating OPEC+ countries (led by Saudi Arabia and Russia) agreed to implement a production adjustment of 411,000 b/d in May 2025. This adjustment combined the originally planned May increment with two previously deferred monthly increments. While OPEC+ cited "healthy market fundamentals" for the decision, the move was viewed by some market participants as a gamble that added supply to an already softening market.

The primary catalyst for the April price collapse was a sudden escalation in global trade tensions. On April 2, 2025, U.S. President Donald Trump signed an Executive Order imposing a 10% universal tariff on imports, which was met on April 4 by a retaliatory 34% tariff from China on U.S. imports. This "tariff war" severely dampened risk sentiment and stoked fears of a global economic slowdown. Although supply-side worries—such as U.S. sanctions targeting Chinese teapot refineries to curb Iranian exports—provided temporary support and led to a brief mid-month rebound, the market remained heavily weighed down by macroeconomic uncertainties.

The demand outlook for 2025 weakened considerably in April. In its April 15, 2025 Oil Market Report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) revised its global oil demand growth forecast for 2025 downward by 300,000 b/d to just 730,000 b/d, pointing directly to the negative economic impacts of escalating trade tensions. This downgrade came despite a robust first quarter where consumption grew by 1.2 million b/d year-on-year. The market was increasingly characterized as comfortably supplied, with non-OPEC+ supply growth continuing to outpace demand.

Prominent financial institutions aggressively lowered their oil price forecasts in April 2025. Goldman Sachs adjusted its Brent crude projections twice in early April, lowering its base-case forecast to $55 per barrel by December 2025 and warning of a sub-$40 extreme scenario for late 2026 due to trade war impacts and OPEC+ supply increases. Similarly, J.P. Morgan lowered its 2025 Brent forecast to $66 per barrel and its WTI forecast to $62 per barrel, citing the Trump administration's active pursuit of lower oil prices and a projected contraction in U.S. shale activity starting in late 2025.
