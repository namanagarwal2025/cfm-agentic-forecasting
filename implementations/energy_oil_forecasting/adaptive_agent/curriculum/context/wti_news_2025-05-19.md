# WTI Market Context — 2025-05-19

WTI crude oil prices experienced a sharp downturn in April and early May 2025, shedding over 20% from early April highs to hit a four-year low near $55–$58 per barrel on May 5. This bearish trend was driven by escalating US tariff threats and larger-than-expected OPEC+ output hikes. However, by mid-May, prices staged a strong recovery, gaining over $8 per barrel from the May 5 low to trade back above $63 per barrel. This rebound was supported by a softer US dollar, positive US inflation data, and speculation surrounding diplomatic talks between the US and Iran.

On the supply side, OPEC+ has significantly accelerated the unwinding of its voluntary production cuts. In early May 2025, the eight core OPEC+ nations (including Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the UAE) agreed to boost output by 411,000 barrels per day (b/d) in June. This followed a similar 411,000 b/d increase implemented for May. Combined with April's adjustments, the group has added 960,000 b/d of supply back to the market, representing a 44% unwinding of the 2.2 million b/d voluntary cuts. OPEC+ defended the move by pointing to healthy market fundamentals and low global inventories, though some analysts suspect the aggressive hikes are aimed at reclaiming market share from high-cost US shale producers.

Geopolitical risks and macroeconomic headwinds continue to pull the market in opposite directions. While ongoing tensions in the Middle East—particularly the threat of conflict involving Israel and Iran—and sanctions on Russia and Venezuela provide a risk premium, trade policy remains a major source of uncertainty. On the demand side, the outlook is softening. The International Energy Agency (IEA) in its May 2025 report projected global oil demand growth to slow to 740,000 b/d in 2025, citing economic headwinds and rising electric vehicle sales. Conversely, OPEC remains more optimistic, holding its 2025 demand growth forecast steady at 1.3 million b/d, while the US Energy Information Administration (EIA) projects growth of 1.0 million b/d.

In response to these shifting dynamics, major institutions have revised their price forecasts downward. In its May 6 Short-Term Energy Outlook (STEO), the EIA lowered its average WTI spot price projections for both 2025 and 2026. TD Economics also trimmed its 2025 WTI average forecast to $62 per barrel (down from $67/bbl), warning that the deepest impacts of the trade war over the next six months could keep prices volatile within a $55–$60/bbl range. Meanwhile, Standard Chartered analysts cautioned that the mid-May price rally was primarily driven by short-term market positioning and macro news flow rather than a fundamental shift to a new, higher stable price range.
