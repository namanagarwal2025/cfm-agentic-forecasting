In September 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices traded in a relatively tight, range-bound pattern, with the active contract swinging between $61.50 and $66.50 per barrel. By late September, WTI was trading around $63.30 per barrel, representing a modest month-on-month decline of about 1.1%. The market experienced a "tug of war" or "smile" pattern throughout the month: prices slid early on due to OPEC+ supply plans and weak demand signals, stabilized mid-month, and briefly spiked late in the month on Russian supply disruptions before easing again as Kurdistan exports resumed.

OPEC+ continued its methodical approach to unwinding production cuts. Following a 547,000 barrels per day (bpd) production hike implemented in September, the eight participating OPEC+ nations met on September 7, 2025, and agreed to a more moderate output increase of 137,000 bpd for October 2025. This adjustment, aimed at gradually returning voluntary cuts, was lower than some market participants had anticipated, providing a brief, restrained support to prices. However, the overall trajectory of rising OPEC+ and non-OPEC supply (from the U.S., Brazil, and Canada) continued to weigh on long-term sentiment.

Geopolitical disruptions and policy moves provided a firm floor against deeper price declines. Key risks included the escalation of the Russia-Ukraine conflict following the suspension of peace talks, Russian refinery strikes, and Moscow's partial ban on diesel exports. In the Middle East, persistent tensions between Israel and Iran, coupled with new U.S. sanctions on Iran and the potential activation of European snapback mechanisms, kept supply and transit risks through the Strait of Hormuz elevated. These factors were partially offset late in the month by the resumption of oil exports from Kurdistan.

The demand outlook remained highly divided. In its September 2025 report, the IEA marginally raised its 2025 global demand growth forecast to 740,000 bpd, citing resilient OECD consumption offset by weak emerging market demand, particularly in China. Conversely, OPEC maintained a much more bullish stance, forecasting demand growth of 1.3 million bpd for 2025. Analyst forecasts reflected growing concerns of a supply glut; a September Reuters poll of 32 economists projected WTI to average $64.39 per barrel in 2025. Meanwhile, the EIA's September Short-Term Energy Outlook warned of massive global inventory builds, predicting Brent would fall to $59/bbl in Q4 2025 and $50/bbl in early 2026, which would imply WTI trading in the mid-to-low $50s.
