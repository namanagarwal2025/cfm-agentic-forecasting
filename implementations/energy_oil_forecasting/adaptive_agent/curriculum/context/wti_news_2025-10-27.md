As of late October 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices are locked in a downward trend, continuing a multi-week decline driven by a growing global oversupply. During the week ending October 17, WTI spot prices slipped to a range of $58.29 to $59.52 per barrel, down from averages in the mid-$60s in September. Although prices experienced a modest technical rebound toward the $62 level during the week of October 20–24, the broader market remains heavily weighed down by rising commercial stockpiles and a flattening forward curve, though the front-end of the curve has managed to remain in backwardation.

OPEC+ has decided to proceed with its plan to gradually unwind voluntary production cuts, approving a modest output increase of 137,000 barrels per day (b/d) for November 2025. This decision comes despite widespread concerns over an already oversupplied market. While some member nations are eager to return shut-in barrels, the group's cautious strategy attempts to balance returning supply against a backdrop of rising global oil inventories, which have built up significantly throughout the year.

Geopolitical supply risks continue to inject volatility into the market, though their upward pressure on prices has recently moderated. Supply concerns are primarily supported by ongoing sanctions on Russian and Iranian oil exports, alongside persistent Ukrainian attacks on Russian energy infrastructure that have disrupted refining activity. However, broader Middle East supply anxieties have eased significantly in late October. Intensified diplomatic efforts surrounding regional conflicts have alleviated fears of major disruptions to flows through key transit corridors, taking some of the geopolitical risk premium out of crude prices.

The demand outlook for the remainder of 2025 and into 2026 remains highly contested among major energy agencies. In its October 2025 report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) revised its 2025 global oil demand growth forecast downward to 710,000 b/d, citing weak macroeconomic conditions and rapid transport electrification. Conversely, OPEC held its 2025 demand growth forecast steady at 1.3 million b/d, pointing to resilient non-OECD consumption. This divergence has led to starkly different analyst projections; the IEA warns of an impending global supply surplus of 2.3 million b/d for 2025, while the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) projects a surplus of 2.1 million b/d in the second half of the year, leaving many analysts bearish on WTI's near-term recovery prospects.
