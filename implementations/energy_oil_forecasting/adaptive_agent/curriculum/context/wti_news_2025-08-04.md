As of early August 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil spot prices hovered in the high $60s to low $70s per barrel, with the Cushing, OK spot price settling at $67.33/b on August 1, 2025. This represented a slight decline from June averages, where the NYMEX WTI front-month contract had risen to $67.33/b (up $6.39 month-on-month). Despite the downward pressure on futures toward the end of July, major crude benchmarks remained in backwardation, supported by tight physical markets and peak summer refinery runs.

OPEC+ production saw a notable upward trajectory through June and July 2025 as the alliance began unwinding voluntary production cuts. OPEC's crude output rose by 263,000 barrels per day (bpd) in July to reach 27.54 million bpd, led primarily by Saudi Arabia, which boosted its production by 170,000 bpd to 9.53 million bpd. This gradual return of supply, alongside higher OPEC+ targets scheduled for August, signaled an easing of the group's tight supply program and contributed to the softening of futures prices.

Geopolitical factors continued to inject volatility into the market, though the war risk premium in the Middle East had somewhat receded by late July following a temporary pause in regional escalations. Nonetheless, concerns persisted regarding potential disruptions to Iranian crude output and shipping routes through the Red Sea and the Strait of Hormuz. Concurrently, escalating tensions between Russia and Ukraine prompted secondary US sanctions targeting buyers of Russian crude in India and China, while European Union discussions focused on lowering the price cap on Russian oil.

The global demand outlook presented a diverging picture between major energy agencies. The International Energy Agency (IEA) in July 2025 forecast global oil demand growth to rise by just 700,000 bpd in 2025—the lowest non-pandemic rate since 2009—citing lackluster emerging market consumption. Conversely, OPEC maintained a more optimistic forecast of 1.3 million bpd growth for 2025, driven by non-OECD demand. In its July Short-Term Energy Outlook, the US Energy Information Administration (EIA) projected that WTI prices would steadily decline through 2026, ending that year at $53/b, which they expected would prompt US producers to scale back drilling and lower domestic production to 13.3 million bpd.
