WTI crude oil prices have experienced a highly volatile start to 2025. In early January, WTI surged alongside Brent, with WTI futures peaking near $80.40 per barrel mid-month, driven by a North American cold snap and new US sanctions. However, this early-month breakout quickly reversed. By late January, WTI prices plunged back toward the yearly open, closing the month around $72.53 per barrel. This downward pressure was largely fueled by escalating trade and tariff uncertainties, particularly President Trump's threats of a 25% tariff on Canada and Mexico, which dampened market sentiment.

On the supply side, the OPEC+ alliance has prioritized market stability in the face of potential oversupply. In December 2024, the coalition agreed to delay its planned 180,000 barrels per day (b/d) production increase—originally scheduled to begin in January 2025—by three months to April 2025. This postponement reflected the group's reluctance to add barrels to a market where prices were hovering in the low-to-mid $70s. However, the United Arab Emirates (UAE) remains exempt from this pause, proceeding with its previously negotiated plan to gradually increase its output by 300,000 b/d over the course of 2025.

Geopolitical risks continue to heavily influence the supply landscape. On January 10, 2025, the US government imposed expansive new sanctions targeting Russia's energy sector, including Gazprom Neft, Surgutneftegaz, and over 160 tankers, which initially choked logistics and boosted prices. Additionally, the market is bracing for a potentially tougher US stance on Iranian oil exports under the incoming Trump administration. While a ceasefire agreement in the Middle East has temporarily capped the geopolitical risk premium, weather-related supply risks remain a near-term focus, as winter freeze-offs threaten North American production, though the prolific Permian Basin has so far avoided major disruptions.

The global demand outlook for 2025 remains moderate but highly sensitive to macroeconomic policies. In its January 2025 report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) projected global oil demand growth to accelerate slightly to 1.05 million b/d in 2025, up from 940,000 b/d in 2024, driven primarily by non-OECD consumption. Major analyst forecasts reflect this balanced-to-surplus outlook; Goldman Sachs, for instance, projects Brent crude to average $76 per barrel in 2025 within a $70–$85 range, noting that high OPEC+ spare capacity will likely cap significant price spikes, while the price elasticity of US shale and OPEC supply will prevent a severe collapse.
