### Price Level and Recent Trend
West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices have experienced a downward trend and sideways volatility in early 2025, with spot prices averaging $71.53 per barrel in February. After trading in the mid-to-high $70s in January, WTI faced downward pressure, breaking below a key support level of $70.00 in late February. This bearish momentum has been driven by a combination of rising non-OPEC supply, growing commercial inventories, and broader macroeconomic concerns, though timespreads have remained relatively resilient.

### OPEC+ Production Decisions
In its virtual meeting on February 3, 2025, the OPEC+ Joint Ministerial Monitoring Committee (JMMC) decided to stick to its established policy, resisting pressure from the U.S. administration to immediately boost output. The alliance confirmed it will proceed with its plan to gradually unwind 2.2 million barrels per day (b/d) of voluntary production cuts starting in April 2025, phasing them out over an 18-month period. The group emphasized the critical importance of member compliance and compensation for past overproduction to maintain market stability.

### Geopolitical Supply Risks
Geopolitical factors continue to influence the market, though some risk premiums have shifted. While easing tensions in the Middle East have slightly reduced the geopolitical premium on Brent, WTI has found support from domestic policy uncertainties. Specifically, proposed U.S. tariffs on Canadian and Mexican energy imports have raised concerns about supply disruptions and increased feedstock costs for U.S. refiners. Additionally, the potential for tighter sanctions on Russian and Iranian oil exports remains a key supply-side risk.

### Demand Outlook and Analyst Forecasts
The demand outlook for 2025 presents a divergence between major forecasting agencies. The International Energy Agency (IEA) projects global oil demand growth to average 1.1 million b/d in 2025, with China and India driving the expansion while OECD demand returns to a structural decline. Conversely, OPEC maintains a more optimistic forecast of 1.4 million b/d growth for the year. In terms of price projections, the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) in its February Short-Term Energy Outlook forecast WTI spot prices to average $70.62 per barrel in 2025 and decline to $62.46 in 2026. Meanwhile, Goldman Sachs expects Brent to trade within a $70–$85 range, averaging $76, noting that high OPEC+ spare capacity will likely cap any significant upward price spikes.
