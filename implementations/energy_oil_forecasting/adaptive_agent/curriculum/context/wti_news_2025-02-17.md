As of mid-February 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices are trading in the low $70s range, hovering around $71 to $72 per barrel. This follows a volatile January where WTI spot prices averaged $75.74 per barrel before embarking on a downward trend, posting consecutive weekly declines into early February. The recent downward pressure is largely driven by macroeconomic uncertainties, including the potential fallout from newly announced US tariffs on steel and aluminum, and ongoing trade tensions with China, which have fanned fears of slowing global economic growth.

On the supply side, OPEC+ decided during its Joint Ministerial Monitoring Committee (JMMC) meeting on February 3, 2025, to stick to its current output policy. The alliance reaffirmed its plan to begin gradually unwinding voluntary production cuts starting in April 2025, stretching the phase-out through the end of 2026. By maintaining this timeline, OPEC+ resisted pressure from the US administration to immediately boost supply to lower prices. The group continues to emphasize strict compliance and compensation for past overproduction, while retaining the flexibility to pause or reverse these output increases if market conditions deteriorate.

Geopolitical factors continue to inject volatility and a risk premium into the market. Tightened US sanctions on Russian and Iranian oil exports have restricted some global flows, driving up demand for Middle Eastern crude and prompting major producers like Saudi Arabia, Iraq, and the UAE to implement significant official selling price hikes. However, these supply-side risks are balanced by trade policy uncertainties. For instance, the newly proposed US tariffs on steel and aluminum could raise costs for domestic shale drillers who rely on specialized imported steel, potentially slowing US production growth.

The demand outlook for 2025 remains mixed. In its February 13, 2025 Oil Market Report, the IEA projected global oil demand growth to average 1.1 million b/d in 2025, up from 870,000 b/d in 2024, with expansion driven almost entirely by China's petrochemical sector and rising demand in India. Conversely, OECD demand is expected to return to a structural decline. Prominent analysts, such as those at Goldman Sachs, forecast Brent crude to trade within a $70 to $85 range (averaging $76) for the year, noting that substantial non-OPEC+ supply growth and high OPEC+ spare capacity will likely cap any major upward price spikes, keeping the market relatively well-supplied.
