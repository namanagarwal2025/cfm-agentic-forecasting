As of late May 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices are hovering in the low $60s per barrel, with the benchmark settling at $61.84 on May 28 and closing at $60.79 on May 30. This represents a period of consolidation after a highly volatile few weeks. Earlier in the month, WTI rebounded slightly from a low of $58.19, which marked its lowest level since February 2021. The broader trend has been decidedly bearish; WTI plummeted nearly 19% in April, recording its steepest monthly loss since late 2021. This downward pressure has been driven by a combination of rising global supply and escalating economic concerns.

The primary catalyst for the recent price slide is an unexpected shift in strategy by OPEC+. During a virtually held meeting on May 3, 2025, the "Voluntary Eight" member countries (led by Saudi Arabia and Russia) agreed to implement a production increase of 411,000 barrels per day (bpd) for June. This decision accelerated the unwinding of their 2.2 million bpd voluntary cuts. Reports indicate that Saudi Arabia is willing to tolerate a prolonged period of lower prices to defend its market share against U.S. shale and align with the U.S. administration's preference for lower energy costs. The group is scheduled to meet again on June 1, 2025, to finalize July output plans, with analysts widely anticipating another 411,000 bpd supply hike.

While physical supply is expanding, geopolitical risks continue to introduce volatility. The market's primary focus has shifted toward trade policy, as newly announced U.S. tariffs—particularly targeting China—threaten to trigger a global trade war and dampen economic growth. Additionally, ongoing sanctions on Russia and Iran remain in place, and a recent escalation in military tensions between India and Pakistan has introduced fresh regional risks to energy infrastructure. Domestically, the start of the Atlantic hurricane season on June 1 poses a seasonal threat, with NOAA forecasting an above-normal season that could disrupt Gulf Coast refining capacity.

The global demand outlook has softened significantly, prompting major agencies to revise their projections downward. In its May 2025 Oil Market Report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) projected that global oil demand growth will slow to 740,000 bpd for the full year 2025, down from earlier estimates, as economic headwinds and surging electric vehicle (EV) sales curb consumption. In response, prominent financial institutions have slashed their price forecasts. Standard Chartered lowered its 2025 WTI average forecast to $58 per barrel, warning that prices could dip to around $50 in the second and third quarters of the year. Meanwhile, the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) in its May Short-Term Energy Outlook projected Brent crude to average $66 per barrel in 2025, and the World Bank estimated Brent would average $64 per barrel for the year due to soft consumption and rising trade barriers.
