### Price Level and Recent Trend
In October 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices continued their downward trajectory, averaging between $60.07 and $60.89 per barrel. This represented a month-on-month decline of approximately 5% and a year-on-year drop of 15%. The decline marked a continuation of a broader bearish trend, with prices plunging more than 25% from their June highs. By mid-to-late October, WTI was testing key technical support levels in the $58 to $60 range. Despite the overall downward pressure, the forward curve remained in backwardation, reflecting relatively tight immediate physical balances even as speculative financial managers maintained a bearish stance.

### OPEC+ Production Decisions
OPEC+ continued its cautious strategy of gradually unwinding its voluntary production cuts. During a virtual meeting on October 5, 2025, eight key OPEC+ members—including Saudi Arabia and Russia—agreed to ease voluntary cuts by a modest 137,000 barrels per day (bpd) starting in November 2025. This followed a similar 137,000 bpd output increase implemented in October. While the alliance justified the adjustments by pointing to low inventory levels and a steady economic outlook, the decision to add barrels to an already well-supplied market added to the downward pressure on prices.

### Geopolitical Supply Risks
Geopolitical factors remained a double-edged sword for the market in October 2025. On one hand, escalating trade tensions between the US and China—highlighted by threats of 100% tariffs on Chinese goods following Beijing's rare-earth export restrictions—stoked fears of a global economic slowdown and depressed prices. On the other hand, supply-side risks provided some support. New US sanctions targeting Russian oil flows (specifically Rosneft and Lukoil) and EU restrictions on Russian LNG imports tightened flows, while ongoing attacks on Russian energy infrastructure reduced domestic refining capacity by an estimated 500,000 bpd. Additionally, potential and existing sanctions on Iranian oil exports continued to inject a risk premium into the market.

### Demand Outlook and Analyst Forecasts
The demand outlook for late 2025 and 2026 remained sluggish, overshadowed by expectations of a looming supply glut. In its October 2025 report, the International Energy Agency (IEA) forecast global oil demand growth of just 700,000 bpd for both 2025 and 2026, citing a harsh macroeconomic climate and rapid transport electrification. This sluggish demand, paired with rising non-OPEC+ production from the US, Brazil, and Guyana, led major agencies to project a significant surplus. BloombergNEF estimated a global oil surplus of 1.2 million bpd for 2025, while the IEA projected an even wider surplus of 2.3 million bpd. Technical analysts noted that while WTI was heavily oversold and testing support, any sustained price recovery would require a significant easing of trade tensions or deeper geopolitical disruptions.
