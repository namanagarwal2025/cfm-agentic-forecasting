As of mid-November 2025, West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil prices are experiencing significant downward pressure, trading in a bearish range between $58 and $61 per barrel. WTI closed at $59.75 on November 7, edged up to $60.09 on November 14, and fell to $58.06 by November 21. This downward trend represents a multi-year low, driven by robust global production—particularly from non-OPEC+ nations—outpacing modest demand growth and leading to rising global inventories. Despite this overarching bearish sentiment, the market remains highly volatile, experiencing sharp, short-lived price spikes whenever geopolitical tensions flare up.

OPEC+ is actively attempting to manage this shifting supply-demand landscape. On October 5, 2025, eight key OPEC+ members (including Saudi Arabia and Russia) agreed to implement a modest production increase of 137,000 barrels per day (bpd) starting in November 2025, representing a cautious, gradual unwinding of their 1.65 million bpd voluntary cuts. However, recognizing seasonal demand weakness and the risk of a supply surplus, the group decided on November 2, 2025, to pause further scheduled production increments for January, February, and March 2026. OPEC+ also continues to emphasize strict compliance, requiring overproducing members like Iraq and Kazakhstan to implement compensation cuts.

Geopolitical risks remain a primary source of market volatility, injecting a persistent risk premium. Key supply concerns include US sanctions targeting Russian oil entities like Rosneft and Lukoil, which threaten to curtail Russian flows, alongside ongoing regional conflicts in the Middle East and Ukraine that expose critical supply chokepoints. On the demand side, outlooks remain divided. The International Energy Agency (IEA) forecasts subdued global demand growth of 790,000 bpd for 2025 and 770,000 bpd for 2026, citing a harsher macroeconomic climate and transport electrification. Conversely, OPEC maintains a more optimistic demand growth forecast of 1.3 million bpd for 2025 and 1.4 million bpd for 2026.

Analyst forecasts generally lean bearish due to expectations of an impending supply surplus. In its November 2025 Short-Term Energy Outlook (STEO), the US Energy Information Administration (EIA) projected that rising global inventories will push crude prices down, with WTI expected to average around $55 per barrel in 2026. Technical analysts highlight that WTI has been trading within a bullish flag pattern, with crucial support holding at $58.1 and a key near-term resistance target at $61.7. Meanwhile, other statistical models project WTI to remain range-bound, forecasting a December 2025 price of approximately $68.00 within a broader $65.00 to $75.00 range.
